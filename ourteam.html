George A. Brooks, PhD
Chairman, CREA
Professor of Integrative Biology, UC Berkeley

Welcome to CREA. The Center for Research and Education on Aging (CREA) is a joint institution between University of California, Berkeley and Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. Our mission is to investigate the basic processes that cause aging, with the goal of improving and extending human healthspan. The Center for Research and Education on Aging integrates the intellectual assets and resources at both the University and the National Laboratory to accomplish this objective. This unique partnership at CREA fosters an environment that supports both basic aging research and educates the next generation of scientists. We are excited to see what the future will bring, and we hope you'll join our mission and vision.

George A. Brooks joined the UC Berkeley faculty in 1971 and currently holds the chair at CREA. Brooks was a pioneer in modern exercise physiology and metabolism, and resposible the "Lactate Shuttle" and "Crossover Concept" hypotheses we know today. Later in the 70's, Brooks challenged the idea that lactic acid causes muscle fatigue after excerise and faced major backlash, including having trouble getting his grants funded. But he soldiered on, conducting more elaborate studies with rats and, years later, moving on to humans. Every time, with every study, his results were consistent with his radical idea.

Eventually, other researchers confirmed the work. Lactic acid is a fuel. And gradually, the thinking among exercise physiologists began to change. "They said, 'You're anaerobic, you need more oxygen,'" Dr. Brooks said. "The scientists were stuck in 1920."
Steven A. Garan
Director of Bioinformatics, CREA
Senior Scientist, LBNL

Steven A. Garan, a long-time guest scientist at Berkeley Lab's Physical Biosciences Division, was named the Director of Bioinformatics at the Center for Research and Education on Aging (CREA) and is a leading scientist in the field of aging research, best known for coining the therm "phenomics" to describe the comprehensive study of phenotypes. While at UC Berkeley, he collaborated for many years with Paola S. Timiras's group on the role of caloric restriction in maintaining estrogen receptor-alpha and IGH-1 receptor immunoreactivity in various nuclei of the mouse hypothalamus.

Garan played a major role in the invention and the development of the Automated Imaging Microscope System (AIMS). Since the completion of the first version in 1998, newer versions were developed, with the final version being completed in 2007. While computer assisted counting technology has been in use for over a decade, advances in an Automated Imaging Microscope System (AIMS), insure 97% accuracy when comparing computer counts to human counts for both nuclear and cytoplasmic stained tissue. More importantly, automated analyses can be customized to quantify populations only within a target anatomical region, thereby reducing the background noise of non-immunoreactive cells when characterizing specific cell populations. His application was used to successfully map the density and distribution of both nuclear expressed estrogen receptor-alpha and cytoplasmicly expressed IGF-1 receptor in specific hypothalamic nuclei. By using this technology, the evaluation of tissue-level biology can be used to establish neuroendocrine biomarkers of aging and analyze the neuroendocrine effects of caloric restriction and gene knockout models that extend the lifespan.
